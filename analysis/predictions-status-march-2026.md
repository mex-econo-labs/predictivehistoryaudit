"Done" is an overstatement, but the trajectory is clearly downward compared to the 2000-2020 boom era.

## HISTORICAL/STRUCTURAL CLAIMS

### 11. "The Plaza Accord — America ordered Japan to destroy its economy"

**Status: OVERSIMPLIFIED. The accord was multilateral and Japan was a willing participant, but the long-term consequences for Japan were severe.**

What actually happened:
- The **Plaza Accord (September 22, 1985)** was signed by France, West Germany, Japan, the UK, and the US at the Plaza Hotel in New York. Its purpose was to **depreciate the U.S. dollar** to reduce the U.S. current account deficit (3.5% of GDP).
- Japan's Finance Minister Takeshita actually **expressed willingness** to accept yen appreciation up to 200 yen/dollar — suggesting Japan was a willing participant, not a coerced one.
- The yen appreciated from **240 to 120 per dollar** over two years, halving the yen earnings of Japanese exporters.

Consequences:
- Japan responded to the resulting recessionary pressure with **massive monetary and fiscal expansion**, which fueled the late-1980s asset bubble.
- When that bubble burst, Japan entered the **"Lost Decade"** (actually lost decades) of deflation and stagnation.
- However, the causal chain is indirect: Plaza Accord (1985) -> yen appreciation -> loose monetary policy response -> bubble (1987-89) -> crash (1990s). The timing does not support a simple "America destroyed Japan's economy" narrative.

The characterization that "America ordered Japan" is inaccurate — it was a negotiated multilateral agreement. The characterization that the accord contributed to Japan's economic malaise has substantial support among economists, but through an indirect chain of policy responses rather than direct destruction.
